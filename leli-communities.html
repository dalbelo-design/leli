The Communities

Five neighborhoods.
One shared future.

Rodeo, Crockett, Pacheco, Vine Hill, and Clyde are unincorporated Contra Costa communities that have long been treated as separate constituencies — too small to matter individually, too disconnected to act collectively. LELI exists to change that.

Each community is distinct — in character, history, and the specific challenges its residents navigate. What they share is a position on the edges of county systems that were rarely designed with them in mind, and a generation of young adults whose expertise has never been fully recognized or put to work.

What They Share

The common ground
between five distinct places.

Each community has its own identity. But they share a set of structural realities that no single community can address alone — and that LELI is designed to surface and connect.

Unincorporated status

None of the five communities are incorporated cities. They are governed at the county level — which means residents often have fewer direct channels to elected representation than residents of incorporated cities just miles away.

Industrial adjacency

All five communities sit in the Marathon and Phillips 66 refinery corridor. The Community Benefits Agreement that funds LELI exists precisely because of the environmental, health, and economic impacts residents of these communities have lived with — and continue to navigate.

Civic access gaps

Educational attainment in these communities ranges from 22% to 34% bachelor's degree or higher — compared to 46% countywide. That gap has historically been used, explicitly or implicitly, to exclude residents from civic decision-making spaces.

Multilingual residents

Each community has a significant foreign-born population whose voices are often missing from county and state policy conversations — not because residents don't have things to say, but because the systems for gathering input were never designed to hear them.

Disconnected signals

A concern raised in Rodeo and the same concern raised in Crockett have historically never been connected — even when they describe identical problems. Each community has been treated as a separate constituency, making collective advocacy nearly impossible to organize.

Untapped expertise

The young adults growing up in these communities hold deep, ground-level knowledge of what is and isn't working — in housing, transportation, environment, health, and economic opportunity. That knowledge has rarely been recognized as the professional asset it is.

Rodeo

Unincorporated Contra Costa County

Rodeo is a small waterfront community perched along San Pablo Bay between the Carquinez Strait and the Hercules hills. It sits in the immediate shadow of the Phillips 66 refinery — a geographic and economic reality that has shaped the community for generations. Residents have long organized around environmental health, and that tradition of civic engagement is a core part of Rodeo's identity.

Rodeo's young adults have grown up watching their community navigate the tension between industrial employment and environmental risk — a tension that demands exactly the kind of nuanced, ground-level policy analysis LELI is designed to produce.

Housing pressures in Rodeo — rising rents, limited affordable stock, and proximity to industrial land uses — connect directly to similar pressures in Crockett, just a few miles up the shoreline. Through the Civic Signals Network, LELI Fellows can surface those connections and make the case for regional responses.

Crockett

Unincorporated Contra Costa County

Crockett is a hillside community overlooking the Carquinez Strait, best known for the C&H Sugar refinery that has anchored its economy and identity for over a century. The town has a strong sense of place and a tight-knit community culture — but like its neighbors, it faces challenges around industrial transition, aging infrastructure, and limited economic mobility for its younger residents.

Crockett's residents have navigated the boom-and-bust cycles of industrial employment, and many families have been there long enough to have opinions — and lived data — about which policy interventions actually work and which ones don't.

Transportation gaps in Crockett — limited transit connections, aging road infrastructure, and limited access to regional job centers — echo concerns raised consistently in Vine Hill and Clyde. When LELI Fellows connect these signals, the resulting findings carry the weight of a regional pattern, not a local complaint.

Pacheco

Unincorporated Contra Costa County

Pacheco is a small unincorporated community situated between Concord and Martinez, shaped by its position at the intersection of industrial, commercial, and residential land uses. It is a community that has often been defined by what surrounds it rather than on its own terms — and whose residents have consistently shown up to public processes to advocate for investment and recognition.

Pacheco has a significant multilingual population, and much of the community input generated by its residents has historically been harder to capture in county systems not designed for language accessibility. The Civic Signals Network's translation capabilities are directly relevant here.

Transportation barriers in Pacheco — including limited access to Contra Costa BART stations and inadequate bus connections — directly impact residents' ability to access employment, healthcare, and civic participation. These barriers surface consistently in community input and connect to nearly identical concerns in Vine Hill and Clyde.

Vine Hill

Unincorporated Contra Costa County

Vine Hill is a residential community in the unincorporated area south of Martinez, known for its working-class character and its proximity to both the Shell Martinez refinery corridor and the Martinez Refining Company operations. Residents have long dealt with air quality concerns and the cumulative health impacts of living in a refinery-adjacent community.

Vine Hill residents have historically been active participants in public comment processes related to refinery operations and environmental permitting — but without the research infrastructure to connect their testimony to patterns across neighboring communities, that participation has had limited systemic impact.

Housing affordability pressures in Vine Hill — particularly for younger residents and renters — mirror dynamics in Rodeo and Crockett. When LELI surfaces these shared patterns, county housing policy conversations become much harder to address as isolated local issues.

Community

Clyde

Unincorporated Contra Costa County

Clyde is one of the smallest of the five communities — a quiet residential enclave situated between Concord and Bay Point, largely defined by the industrial infrastructure that surrounds it and the limited civic visibility that has come with its small size and unincorporated status. Despite its size, Clyde's residents experience many of the same structural challenges as its larger neighbors.

Clyde's young adults are among those least likely to have had meaningful access to county civic processes — not because they lack interest or insight, but because the invitation has rarely been extended and the infrastructure for participation has rarely been designed with them in mind.

Transportation isolation in Clyde is particularly acute — limited transit options make accessing employment, education, and even civic participation events in larger neighboring cities difficult. This isolation compounds across nearly every other issue residents face, from healthcare access to economic opportunity.

The Power of Connection

What becomes possible when these communities see each other.

Isolated concerns become regional patterns. Local complaints become evidence of systemic failure. And coordinated advocacy becomes possible where scattered testimony never could have moved the needle.

Rodeo + Crockett + Vine Hill

A housing pattern, not a local problem

Rising rents, limited affordable stock, and displacement pressure have been raised separately in all three communities for years. When LELI Fellows connect these signals, the finding changes from "Rodeo has a housing problem" to "the refinery corridor has a housing crisis that requires a regional response."

Policy implication: County housing policy and state affordability programs can be engaged with coordinated community evidence across three jurisdictions simultaneously.
Pacheco + Vine Hill + Clyde

A transit corridor, not isolated gaps

Limited bus service, lack of safe pedestrian infrastructure, and poor connections to BART have been documented in all three communities separately. Connected, they describe a transit corridor where residents consistently can't get to work, school, or healthcare without a car.

Policy implication: A coordinated case can be made to Contra Costa Transportation Authority for corridor-level investment rather than isolated stop improvements.
Rodeo + Crockett + Vine Hill

Cumulative health impacts, not individual complaints

Air quality concerns, childhood asthma rates, and refinery-adjacent health impacts have been raised in every community bordering the refinery corridor. Separately they're anecdote. Together they're evidence — the kind that supports environmental justice advocacy at the state and federal level.

Policy implication: Bay Area Air Quality Management District and CalEPA can be engaged with a multi-community cumulative impact analysis, not just individual monitoring complaints.
All 5 communities

A generation ready to lead — if given the infrastructure

Across all five communities, young adults ages 18–26 are navigating the same structural barriers to civic participation: no paid pathway in, no research tools, no institutional relationships. LELI is the infrastructure that changes that — for all five at once.

What this means: The first cohort of ten Fellows represents all five communities simultaneously — creating the first shared civic research capacity these neighborhoods have ever had.

Are you from one of these communities?

The 2026–27 Fellowship is open to young adults ages 18–26 with ties to Rodeo, Crockett, Pacheco, Vine Hill, or Clyde. Your experience of these places is exactly what we're looking for.
